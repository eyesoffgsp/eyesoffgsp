Digital Privacy

This page is a side project intended to help you take back your digital privacy from Big Tech (and by extension the government in some ways), one step at a time.
Why data privacy is important:

If the possibility that your phone is listening to you 100% of the time and using your conversations to show you advertisements about things you've talked about creeps you out, that may be reason enough. There's also a personal safety aspect in some cases. Maybe you like the idea of standing up to Big Tech; if enough people make the same few small changes to their data habits, it could really get some attention.

It's important to understand how data has value. It may not seem like that big of a deal to blindly agree to the privacy policy on something as mundane as a (internet connected) thermostat. Who cares if some company knows what temperature my house is at right now? But they're making money off selling your data to data brokers, and you aren't getting a cut. In fact, you're probably paying them to do it through a subscription. There are several things driving the value behind any and all data no matter how insignificant it may seem, including: training AI, pushing targeted ads, and law enforcement bypassing the 4th Amendment.

"Targeted ads" does not mean showing an ad for beer during a football game. This is based on demographics, not on an individual viewer's preferences. Targeted ads go down the level of the individual. If you set your thermostat to 68 degrees in the summer, you're probably more likely to buy a USB-C powered mini travel fan than someone who sets theirs to 75 degrees. Or imagine a young girl who deletes a picture on Facebook being flagged as "insecure" and subsequently gets shown ads for beauty products. Yes, Facebook admitted to doing that when they were subpoenaed by Congress. It should come as no surprise that corporations and Big Tech are willing to put profits before ethics. If you don't want to be a subject in this system, you've come to the right place.

How to use this page:

As technology advances and new products and software come out, we get more and more conveniences in life. But with each convenience, we give away a small piece of our privacy. Not by definition, but because of corporate greed and Big Tech sneaking in anti-consumer and anti-privacy practices right under our noses, until we become educated and take steps against it. This page intends to help you do just that.

In some cases, you may need to give up small conveniences. This will be an adjustment, but you used to live without it and you can do it again if it's worth it to you. Soon enough you'll get used to doing it the old school way and it won't be a big deal anymore. In other cases, there are safe alternatives and it's just a matter of taking the time (or money) to make the switch.

This can be manageable if you make one small change at a time as you have time. Since there's so many ways our data is being collected, one change on its own won't have much of an impact. But many small changes added together eventually makes a bigger impact (have you read Atomic Habits?).

When relevant, I will recommend alternative products that I know of. However, there may be many more options out there with different pros and cons. When I'm trying to learn about options myself, I do an internet search for, say, "best internet browser for privacy reddit" and see if there's a forum discussing this topic already. Reading through the comments should give you an idea of options and their pros and cons. This is a community based approach rather than just trusting the word of a single influencer out there who likely has ulterior financial motives.

Open-source vs closed-source:

This is an important topic to understand when it comes to taking back your digital privacy. Often, steps involve switching from a corporation's proprietary product to a non-proprietary, community-based solution.

Closed-source software or hardware means a company can build in whatever features they want running in the background and you essentially have no way of knowing. Any closed-source, internet connected device could be constantly collecting your data and sending it to the company's servers to then sell to a data broker.

Open-source software or hardware is usually developed by engineers in their free time and is made available for anyone to review and use (generally for free). Anyone can contribute and build off of it, or look for security vulnerabilities and help patch them. Imagine what hundreds of software developers across the globe with varying expertise can do when they come together on a project that they're passionate about compared to a corporation with profit-margins and employees who work to live.

URL (link) Trackers

A URL is a link that you click to go to a website. Websites will often imbed tracking info into the link that gets shared so they can collect data about anyone who clicks on the link. Fortunately, this is easy to spot and remove once you know what to look for.

Here's an example of a "dirty" URL that has trackers in it:

https://www.foxcarolina.com/2026/05/13/spartanburg-co-councilman-appear-court-motion-hearing-court-records-say/?fbclid=IwdGRzaARy6dRjbGNrBHLpxmV4dG4DYWVtAjExAHNydGMGYXBwX2lkDDM1MDY4NTUzMTcyOAABHtLqc9g3AmRT5IHctHs_DyQeGiAQS9ujKM8lccVgw66ZFXgUyvNZo5W--NJG_aem_Y0_0Z9dpmeFCW1GzVOK3xg&sfnsn=mo

Now here's the "clean" version of the same link:

https://www.foxcarolina.com/2026/05/13/spartanburg-co-councilman-appear-court-motion-hearing-court-records-say/

The important part of the URL always comes first and the tracking info is just tacked onto the end. As a rule of thumb, you can start by only copying the human-readable part of the link and if it doesn't work add the next section until it does work. There's no harm in only copying part of the link, you will just get an error page. Another clue to look for is that the trackers usually begin with ? or = but not always. The random series of numbers and letters in the tracker may seem meaningless to us, but they translate to personal info (such as your IP address, device type and location, timestamps, the Google search that led you to the website, etc.) that the website is programmed to understand.

As an exercise, copy and paste each line one at a time into another tab on your web browser to see what happens:

https://www.foxcarolina.com/
https://www.foxcarolina.com/2026/05/13
https://www.foxcarolina.com/2026/05/13/spartanburg-co-councilman-appear-court-motion-hearing-court-records-say/

When someone sends you a link, be sure to remove the trackers first. I usually copy the link into notes on my phone first, delete the part that looks like a tracker, and copy whats left of the link and paste that into my internet browser. It's also a good idea to clean up links before you send them to someone or post them online. Trackers are usually added when you click the "share" or "copy link" button on a website, so it's best to copy the URL directly out of the bar on your web browser (for example if you wanted to text someone a link to a YouTube video or Facebook post). However, that method isn't foolproof. If you paste the clean example below into your browser, you'll notice after the page loads that the URL in your browser bar gets a tracker added to it. That doesn't mean that you've been tracked, it just means they've preemptively added the tracker in case you decide to share the link in the browser bar with someone else.
